# Engagement Techniques

## Hook Formulas (Opening 1-2 Sentences)

The hook is the single most important element for engagement. Readers decide within 3 seconds whether to continue.

### 1. Provocative Question
Challenges assumptions or sparks curiosity.
- "What if the 'free' plan is actually costing you $500/month in lost productivity?"
- "When was the last time a marketing email actually changed what you bought?"
- "Why do 73% of content marketers say they're creating more content than ever — but getting worse results?"

### 2. Surprising Statistic
Data-driven opening with exact numbers (not vague "many" or "most").
- "Companies that blog 16+ times per month get 3.5x more traffic than those posting 0-4 times."
- "The average person encounters 6,000-10,000 ads per day — and remembers fewer than 10."
- "47% of buyers consume 3-5 pieces of content before engaging with a sales rep."

### 3. Bold Statement
Counterintuitive or contrarian claim that demands attention.
- "Most landing pages lose half their visitors in the first three seconds."
- "SEO isn't about ranking #1 anymore. It's about owning the SERP."
- "Your content calendar is probably making your content worse."

### 4. Specific Scenario
Paints a vivid picture the reader recognizes from their own life.
- "It's 3 PM on a Thursday. You're staring at a blank Google Doc, cursor blinking, with a blog post due tomorrow. Your keyword research is a mess of contradicting data..."
- "You just spent six weeks building the perfect landing page. Beautiful design, compelling copy, flawless mobile experience. Then you check analytics: 2.1% conversion rate."

### 5. Contrarian Claim
Opposes conventional wisdom with evidence.
- "Everything you've been told about keyword density is wrong."
- "Long-form content doesn't always win. Here's when shorter is better."
- "Stop A/B testing your headlines. There's a better way."

## Introduction Framework: APP Method

After the hook, structure the introduction with:

**Agree** — Acknowledge the reader's current situation, frustration, or belief:
"If you're like most content teams, you're drowning in keyword data but starving for actual strategy."

**Promise** — State what they'll gain from reading:
"In this guide, you'll learn the exact framework we used to increase organic traffic 340% in 6 months — without publishing more content."

**Preview** — Brief roadmap of what's coming:
"We'll cover the 3-pillar content audit, the priority scoring system, and the refresh playbook that turned our declining pages into traffic magnets."

**Rules**:
- Primary keyword appears in first 100 words
- Total introduction: 100-150 words
- Establishes credibility (data, experience, results)
- Flows naturally from hook to body

## Mini-Stories (2-3 Per Article)

Stories make content memorable. Research shows facts wrapped in narrative are 22x more memorable.

### Structure (50-150 words each)
1. **Character** — specific name (even if fictional): "Sarah, a content manager at a B2B SaaS startup"
2. **Situation** — concrete details: dates, numbers, specific tools, real metrics
3. **Tension** — the problem or challenge faced
4. **Resolution** — what happened, what they learned, what changed
5. **Lesson** — connects back to the article's point

### Placement Strategy
- **Story 1** (intro/early section): Hook readers with a relatable scenario
- **Story 2** (middle): Re-engage skimmers who might be losing interest
- **Story 3** (near conclusion): Reinforce the main point with a success example

### Example
"Last March, Marcus and his team at Relay were publishing four blog posts a week. Traffic was growing — slowly. Then they stopped publishing entirely for two weeks and ran the content audit framework we're about to share. When they resumed, they published just one post per week — but each was backed by the priority scoring system. Within 90 days, organic traffic was up 180%. The lesson? Volume without strategy is just noise."

## Contextual CTAs (2-3 Per Article)

Embedded CTAs convert 121% better than end-only CTAs.

### Soft CTA (After First Major Value Section)
Placed after the reader has received genuine value. Low commitment.
- "Want to see this framework in action? [Watch the 5-minute walkthrough →]"
- "Grab the free template we use for this process: [Download the SEO Audit Sheet]"

### Medium CTA (After Proof/Comparison Section)
Placed after showing results, case studies, or comparisons. Medium commitment.
- "Ready to try this with your own content? [Start your free 14-day trial]"
- "See how this compares to your current workflow: [Run a free audit]"

### Strong CTA (Conclusion)
Final call to action with supporting text and risk reversal.
- "[Start Your Free Trial →] — No credit card required. Set up in 2 minutes."
- "[Get the Complete SEO Playbook] — Used by 5,000+ content teams. Free download."

### Rules
- First CTA within first 500 words
- Each CTA is contextually connected to surrounding content
- Vary format (inline link, bold callout, button-style block)
- Never use generic anchor text ("click here", "learn more")
- Make specific: reader should know exactly what they get
